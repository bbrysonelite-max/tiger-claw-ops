## Architecture Decision: Two Keys, One Bot

Every Tiger Bot ships with two LLM connections. This is non-negotiable after the Zoom incident.

| Key | Purpose | Model | Cost | Lifespan |
|-----|---------|-------|------|----------|
| **Fallback Key** (yours) | Onboarding interviews, error handling, key rotation prompts | Gemini 2.0 Flash | ~$0.01/day per bot | Permanent — hardwired, never removed |
| **Primary Key** (customer's) | All prospecting, nurturing, daily operations | Customer's choice (OpenAI, Qwen, Gemini, OpenRouter) | Customer pays | Activated at rotation, replaces trial key for operations |

**The rule:** The fallback key is always present. It handles onboarding (first 72 hours) and error recovery (forever). When the customer's key is active and working, the fallback key sleeps. When the customer's key fails, the fallback key wakes up for exactly one purpose: tell the customer what happened and how to fix it.

**Cost exposure on your Gemini fallback key:** Gemini 2.0 Flash pricing is $0.10 per 1M input tokens, $0.40 per 1M output tokens [1]. A full onboarding interview (both interviews, all 12 questions plus conversation) uses roughly 15,000 tokens total. That is $0.002 per customer onboarding. At 100 customers, your total onboarding LLM cost is $0.20. Error handling messages are even cheaper — a single "your key died" message is under 500 tokens.

## The State Machine

The bot's brain is a finite state machine. Each customer has a `state` that determines what the bot does when it receives a message. States move forward, never backward (except on explicit reset).

```
IDLE → WELCOME → INTERVIEW_1 → INTERVIEW_2 → KEY_PROMPT → ACTIVE → ERROR_RECOVERY
```

### State Definitions

| State | Trigger | What the Bot Does | LLM Key Used | Next State |
|-------|---------|-------------------|--------------|------------|
| `IDLE` | Bot created, no /start yet | Nothing. Waits. | None | `WELCOME` on /start |
| `WELCOME` | Customer taps /start | Greets by name, explains what Tiger Bot does, asks if ready to begin | Fallback (Gemini) | `INTERVIEW_1` on "yes" or any affirmative |
| `INTERVIEW_1` | Customer says yes | Asks 6 questions about the customer, one at a time, conversationally | Fallback (Gemini) | `INTERVIEW_2` on completion |
| `INTERVIEW_2` | Interview 1 complete | Asks 6 questions about their ideal customer, one at a time | Fallback (Gemini) | `KEY_PROMPT` on completion |
| `KEY_PROMPT` | Interview 2 complete | Explains the 72-hour trial, offers to help set up their own key | Fallback (Gemini) | `ACTIVE` on key rotation or after 72h auto-transition |
| `ACTIVE` | Key rotated or trial period | Full operations — prospecting, nurturing, daily reports | Primary (customer's key) | `ERROR_RECOVERY` if key fails |
| `ERROR_RECOVERY` | Customer's key fails | Tells customer what happened, provides fix instructions, offers `/setkey` | Fallback (Gemini) | `ACTIVE` on successful key replacement |

## State 1: WELCOME

When the customer taps `/start`, the bot sends this message. It is not generated by the LLM — it is a fixed template with variable substitution. This ensures consistency and zero LLM cost for the greeting.

```
Hey {first_name}! 🐯

I'm your Tiger Bot — your personal AI prospecting machine.

I'm going to help you find customers, nurture them, and close deals 
while you focus on what you do best.

But first, I need to learn about you and your business. 
It'll take about 5 minutes — just answer naturally, 
like you're talking to a friend.

Ready to get started?
```

**Implementation note:** `{first_name}` comes from the tenant record created at purchase. If the name is not available (Stripe didn't capture it), the bot says "Hey there!" instead.

The bot waits for any response. Any affirmative (yes, yeah, sure, let's go, ready, ok) triggers transition to `INTERVIEW_1`. If the customer says something else, the bot responds conversationally (via Gemini) and asks again.

---

## State 2: INTERVIEW_1 — "Who Are You?"

This is a conversational interview, not a form. The bot asks one question at a time, waits for the answer, acknowledges it naturally, then asks the next question. The LLM (Gemini fallback key) powers the conversational layer — it receives the question template, the customer's answer, and generates a natural acknowledgment before asking the next question.

### The 6 Questions

| # | Question | What We Extract | Storage Field |
|---|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| 1 | "What's your full name — first and family name?" | Full name | `customer.full_name` |
| 2 | "What's the best phone number to reach you?" | Phone number | `customer.phone` |
| 3 | "Tell me about your business — what do you do?" | Business type, industry, description | `customer.business_type`, `customer.business_description` |
| 4 | "How long have you been doing this?" | Experience level (months/years) | `customer.experience_years` |
| 5 | "What's your mission? When you picture success, what does it look like?" | Mission statement, success metrics | `customer.mission` |
| 6 | "How are you finding customers right now? What's working, what's not?" | Current methods, pain points | `customer.current_methods`, `customer.pain_points` |

### LLM System Prompt for Interview 1

The LLM receives this system prompt during Interview 1. It does not change between questions — only the conversation history grows.

```
You are Tiger Bot, a friendly and professional AI assistant helping 
a new customer get set up. You are conducting an onboarding interview.

Your job:
1. Ask the current question naturally (not robotically)
2. When the customer answers, acknowledge their answer warmly 
   and briefly (1-2 sentences max)
3. Then ask the next question
4. If the answer is unclear or incomplete, ask a gentle follow-up
5. Never skip a question
6. Never lecture or give unsolicited advice
7. Keep it conversational — like a smart colleague, not a survey

Current question number: {question_number}
Current question: {question_text}
Questions remaining: {remaining_count}

Previous conversation:
{conversation_history}
```

### Data Extraction

After each answer, a separate LLM call (not shown to the customer) extracts structured data from the natural language response. This is a JSON extraction prompt:

```
Extract the following from this customer response. 
Return ONLY valid JSON, no explanation.

Customer said: "{customer_message}"
Question was about: "{question_topic}"

Return: {
  "extracted_value": "the key information",
  "confidence": "high/medium/low",
  "needs_followup": true/false,
  "followup_reason": "why, if needed"
}
```

If `needs_followup` is true and `confidence` is low, the bot asks a clarifying question before moving on.

### Interview 1 Completion

When all 6 questions are answered, the bot generates a **Customer Briefing** — a `.md` file stored in the customer's persistent memory directory.

```markdown
# Customer Briefing: {full_name}
Generated: {timestamp}

## Identity
- **Name:** {full_name}
- **Phone:** {phone}
- **Market:** {detected_country/region}

## Business
- **Type:** {business_type}
- **Description:** {business_description}
- **Experience:** {experience_years} years

## Mission
{mission}

## Current State
- **Methods:** {current_methods}
- **Pain Points:** {pain_points}

## Bot Configuration
- **Language:** {detected_language}
- **Timezone:** {detected_timezone}
- **Tone:** {inferred_tone_preference}
```

The bot then says:

```
Great — I've got a solid picture of you and your business, {first_name}.

Now I need to understand who you're looking for. 
Who's your ideal customer? This helps me know exactly 
who to hunt for.

Let's keep going — 6 more questions.
```

Transition to `INTERVIEW_2`.

## State 3: INTERVIEW_2 — "Who Is Your Ideal Customer?"

Same conversational approach. Same LLM system prompt structure. Different questions.

### The 6 Questions

| # | Question | What We Extract | Storage Field |
|---|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| 1 | "Describe your ideal customer. Who are they?" | Demographics, psychographics | `icp.description` |
| 2 | "What problem do they have that you solve?" | Pain points, needs | `icp.pain_points` |
| 3 | "Where do they hang out online? Facebook groups, LINE, Instagram, forums?" | Channels, platforms | `icp.channels` |
| 4 | "What words or phrases would they use when looking for help?" | Keywords, search terms | `icp.keywords` |
| 5 | "What triggers them to finally take action and buy?" | Buying triggers, urgency signals | `icp.triggers` |
| 6 | "Is there anyone I should avoid? Anyone who's NOT a good fit?" | Exclusion criteria | `icp.exclusions` |

On completion, the bot generates an **ICP Briefing**:

```markdown
# Ideal Customer Profile: {customer_name}'s Target

## Who They Are
{description}

## Their Problem
{pain_points}

## Where to Find Them
{channels}
- Primary platforms: {platform_list}
- Keywords to monitor: {keyword_list}

## Buying Triggers
{triggers}

## Exclusions
{exclusions}

## Prospecting Configuration
- **Monitor keywords:** {keyword_list}
- **Monitor platforms:** {platform_list}
```

The bot then says:

```
Perfect, {first_name}. I now know who you are and who you're 
looking for. That's everything I need to start hunting.

One more thing before I get to work — let's talk about 
your AI brain for a second.
```

Transition to `KEY_PROMPT`.

## State 4: KEY_PROMPT

This state handles the 72-hour trial explanation and API key rotation. The bot uses the Gemini fallback key for this entire conversation.

### Initial Message

```
Right now, I'm running on a starter brain that {your_name} 
provided for your first 72 hours — totally free.

After that, you'll need your own AI key. It costs about 
$1-10/month depending on which provider you pick, 
and it takes about 2 minutes to set up.

Want me to walk you through it now, or would you rather 
do it later? Either way, I'll remind you before the 
72 hours are up.
```

### If "Now"

The bot walks them through the provider selection and key setup — the same walkthrough content from the webpage, but delivered conversationally in Telegram. It ends with:

```
When you have your key, just type:

/setkey YOUR_API_KEY_HERE

I'll verify it works and switch over instantly. 
No downtime, no interruption.
```

### If "Later"

```
No problem. I'll remind you at 48 hours and again at 
64 hours. You've got plenty of time.

In the meantime — I'm going to start working for you 
right now with the starter brain.
```

### The Timer System

| Hour | Action |
|------|--------|
| 0 | Trial begins. Bot starts operating on fallback Gemini key. |
| 24 | No reminder yet. Let them experience the bot working. |
| 48 | First reminder: "Hey {name}, just a heads up — your free trial brain has 24 hours left. Want me to help you set up your own key?" |
| 64 | Second reminder: "8 hours left on your starter brain. After that, I'll need your own key to keep working. It takes 2 minutes — want to do it now?" |
| 72 | Final: "Your starter brain trial has ended. I need your own API key to continue prospecting and nurturing. Type /setkey YOUR_KEY to get me back to work. I'll be right here waiting." |
| 72+ | Bot enters `KEY_PENDING` sub-state. It responds to `/setkey` only. All other messages get: "I'm paused until you add your API key. Type /setkey YOUR_KEY and I'll be back in action instantly." |

### The `/setkey` Command

When a customer types `/setkey sk-abc123...`, the bot:

1. Detects the key prefix to identify the provider (sk- = OpenAI, sk-or- = OpenRouter, AIza = Gemini, sk-ant- = Anthropic)
2. Makes a minimal test call to the provider's API (a simple "say hello" completion)
3. If valid: stores the key encrypted in the customer's config, switches to primary key, confirms success
4. If invalid: tells the customer exactly what went wrong ("That key starts with 'sk-' so I tried OpenAI, but they rejected it. It might be expired. Check your OpenAI dashboard.")

## State 5: ACTIVE

The bot is now fully operational on the customer's primary key. This is where the prospecting, nurturing, and daily operations happen. The specification for what the bot does in ACTIVE state is the flywheel system — that is a separate build spec. For this document, the key point is:

**The fallback Gemini key remains loaded but dormant.** It activates only if the primary key fails.

---

## State 6: ERROR_RECOVERY

When any LLM call using the customer's primary key returns an error (401 unauthorized, 429 rate limit, 500 server error, network timeout), the bot:

1. Catches the error silently — the customer does not see a stack trace or technical error
2. Switches to the fallback Gemini key
3. Sends a human-readable error message based on the error type:

| Error | Message to Customer |
|-------|-------------------|
| 401 / Invalid key | "Your AI key stopped working — it might be expired or revoked. Check your {provider} dashboard and type /setkey with a new key." |
| 429 / Rate limit | "Your AI key hit its usage limit for this period. This usually resets in an hour. I'll try again automatically, or you can upgrade your plan with {provider}." |
| 402 / No credits | "Your {provider} account ran out of credits. Add more at {provider_dashboard_url} and I'll pick right back up." |
| 500 / Server error | "Your AI provider ({provider}) is having technical issues right now. This isn't on your end. I'll keep trying every 15 minutes and let you know when it's back." |
| Timeout | "I couldn't reach {provider} — might be a temporary network issue. I'll retry in 5 minutes." |

4. For 429 and 500 errors, the bot retries automatically with exponential backoff (5 min, 15 min, 30 min, 1 hour)
5. For 401 and 402 errors, the bot waits for `/setkey` — no automatic retry since the key itself is the problem
6. The bot continues to respond to direct messages using the Gemini fallback key during error recovery, but only for error-related conversation — not for prospecting or nurturing

**The customer never sees a dead bot again.**

## Data Storage

Each customer's data lives in a structured directory. This aligns with the OpenClaw `.md` briefing file approach.

```
/customers/{customer_id}/
  briefing.md          ← Generated from Interview 1
  icp.md               ← Generated from Interview 2
  config.json          ← API key (encrypted), provider, state, timers
  conversation.log     ← Full interview transcript
  prospects/           ← Prospect data (populated in ACTIVE state)
  nurture/             ← Nurture campaign state (populated in ACTIVE state)
```

The `config.json` structure:

```json
{
  "customer_id": "tiger_5g6swcaw",
  "telegram_chat_id": 123456789,
  "state": "ACTIVE",
  "created_at": "2026-02-12T10:00:00Z",
```

## Implementation Sequence

This is the build order. Each step depends on the one before it.

### Step 1: The /start Handler (Day 1)

Add a `/start` command handler to the gateway's bot message processor. When a message comes in with `/start`, look up the `customer_id` from the `chat_id`, set state to `WELCOME`, send the welcome message template. This requires zero LLM calls — it is pure template substitution.

**Test:** Tap /start on any of the 9 existing bots. You should see the welcome message with the customer's name.

### Step 2: The Conversation Engine (Day 1-2)

Build the conversation state machine. Each incoming message is routed based on the customer's current `state`. The engine calls Gemini with the appropriate system prompt, conversation history, and current question. It stores the response and advances the question counter.

**Test:** Complete Interview 1 on your own bot (Tiger_Brent_bot). Verify the briefing.md is generated with your actual answers.

### Step 3: Interview 2 + Briefing Generation (Day 2)

Same engine, different questions. Generate the ICP briefing on completion.

**Test:** Complete Interview 2. Verify icp.md is generated. Verify the bot transitions to KEY_PROMPT.

### Step 4: The /setkey Command (Day 2-3)

Implement key detection, validation, storage, and switching. This is the most security-sensitive piece — keys must be encrypted at rest.

**Test:** Type `/setkey` with a valid OpenAI key. Verify the bot confirms success and switches providers. Type `/setkey` with a garbage string. Verify the bot rejects it gracefully.

### Step 5: Error Recovery (Day 3)

Wrap all primary-key LLM calls in a try/catch that falls back to Gemini. Map error codes to human-readable messages.

**Test:** Set a deliberately expired API key. Verify the bot catches the error, switches to Gemini, and tells the customer what happened.

### Step 6: Timer System (Day 3)

Implement the 48h/64h/72h reminder schedule. This can be a simple cron job or a scheduled message queue.

**Test:** Set a trial expiry to 5 minutes from now. Verify the reminders fire and the bot pauses at expiry.

## What This Does NOT Cover

This specification covers the bot's brain — the conversation from `/start` through onboarding to `ACTIVE` state, plus error recovery. It does **not** cover:

- What the bot does in `ACTIVE` state (prospecting, nurturing, daily reports) — that is the flywheel spec
- Infrastructure fixes (Redis, Supabase, Upstash) — that is Priority 2
- The automation chain from Stripe to bot delivery — that is Priority 2
- The dashboard connecting to real data instead of mock data — that is Priority 3
- Hive learning between bots — that is Priority 3

Those are separate build specs. This one gets the bot talking.
